<p>The <b>Intel 8085</b> ("<i>eighty-eighty-five</i>") is an <a href="/wiki/8-bit" title="8-bit">8-bit</a> <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> introduced by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> in 1977.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It was backward <a href="/wiki/Binary_code_compatibility" title="Binary code compatibility">binary compatible</a> with the more-famous <a href="/wiki/Intel_8080" title="Intel 8080">Intel 8080</a> (only adding a few minor instructions) but required less supporting hardware, thus allowing simpler and less expensive <a href="/wiki/Microcomputer" title="Microcomputer">microcomputer</a> systems to be built.</p>
<p>The "5" in the model number came from the fact that the 8085 requires only a +5-<a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">Volt</a> (V) power supply by using <a href="/wiki/Depletion-load_NMOS_logic" title="Depletion-load NMOS logic">depletion mode</a> transistors, rather than requiring the +5&#160;V, −5&#160;V and +12&#160;V supplies the 8080 needed. This is similar to the competing <a href="/wiki/Z80" title="Z80" class="mw-redirect">Z80</a> (also 8080-derived) introduced the year before. These processors were sometimes used in computers running the <a href="/wiki/CP/M_operating_system" title="CP/M operating system" class="mw-redirect">CP/M operating system</a>.</p>
<p>The Intel 8085 required at least an external ROM and RAM and an 8 bit address latch (both latches combined in the Intel 8755 2Kx8 EPROM / 2x8 I/O, Intel 8155 256-byte RAM and 22 I/O and 14 bit programmable Timer/Counter) so cannot technically be called a <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontroller</a>.This microprocessor has a 16 bit address bus and an 8 bit data bus.</p>
<p>Both designs (8080/8085) were eclipsed for desktop computers by the compatible <a href="/wiki/Zilog_Z80" title="Zilog Z80">Zilog Z80</a>, which took over most of the CP/M computer market as well as taking a share of the booming <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">home computer</a> market in the early-to-mid-1980s.</p>
<p>The 8085 had a long life as a controller. Once designed into such products as the <a href="/wiki/DECtape" title="DECtape">DECtape</a> controller and the <a href="/wiki/VT102" title="VT102" class="mw-redirect">VT102</a> video terminal in the late 1970s, it served for new production throughout the life span of those products (generally longer than the product life of desktop computers).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Intel_8085_arch.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Intel_8085_arch.svg/220px-Intel_8085_arch.svg.png" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Intel_8085_arch.svg/330px-Intel_8085_arch.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Intel_8085_arch.svg/440px-Intel_8085_arch.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="975" data-file-height="881" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Intel_8085_arch.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
i8085 microarchitecture.</div>
</div>

<p>The 8085 is a conventional <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_architecture" title="Von Neumann architecture">von Neumann</a> design based on the Intel 8080. Unlike the 8080 it does not multiplex state signals onto the data bus, but the 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Bus_(computing)" title="Bus (computing)">data bus</a> was instead multiplexed with the lower part of the 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Address_bus" title="Address bus">address bus</a> to limit the number of pins to 40. Pin No. 40 is used for the power supply (+5&#160;V) and pin No. 20 for ground. Pin No. 39 is used as the hold pin. Pins No. 15 to No. 8 are generally used for address buses. The processor was designed using <a href="/wiki/NMOS_logic" title="NMOS logic">nMOS</a> circuitry and the later "H" versions were implemented in Intel's enhanced nMOS process called HMOS, originally developed for fast static RAM products. Only a 5 volt supply is needed, like competing processors and unlike the 8080. The 8085 uses approximately 6,500 <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The 8085 incorporates the functions of the 8224 (clock generator) and the 8228 (system controller), increasing the level of integration. A downside compared to similar contemporary designs (such as the Z80) was the fact that the buses required demultiplexing; however, address latches in the Intel 8155, 8355, and 8755 memory chips allowed a direct interface, so an 8085 along with these chips was almost a complete system.</p>
<p>The 8085 has extensions to support new interrupts, with three maskable vectored interrupts (RST 7.5, RST 6.5 and RST 5.5), one <a href="/wiki/Non-maskable_interrupt" title="Non-maskable interrupt">non-maskable interrupt</a> (TRAP), and one externally serviced interrupt (INTR). The RST n.5 interrupts refer to actual pins on the processor, a feature which permitted simple systems to avoid the cost of a separate interrupt controller. Interrupts are enabled by the EI instruction and disabled by the DI instruction.</p>
<p>Like the 8080, the 8085 can accommodate slower memories through externally generated <a href="/wiki/Wait_state" title="Wait state">wait states</a> (pin 35, READY), and has provisions for <a href="/wiki/Direct_Memory_Access" title="Direct Memory Access" class="mw-redirect">Direct Memory Access</a> (DMA) using HOLD and HLDA signals (pins 39 and 38). An improvement over the 8080 was that the 8085 can itself drive a <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric_crystal" title="Piezoelectric crystal" class="mw-redirect">piezoelectric crystal</a> directly connected to it, and a built in clock generator generates the internal high amplitude <a href="/wiki/Two-phase_clock" title="Two-phase clock" class="mw-redirect">two-phase clock</a> signals at half the crystal frequency (a 6.14&#160;MHz crystal would yield a 3.07&#160;MHz clock, for instance).</p>
<p>The 8085 is a <a href="/wiki/Binary_compatible" title="Binary compatible" class="mw-redirect">binary compatible</a> follow up on the 8080, using the same basic <a href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> as the 8080. Only a few minor instructions were new to the 8085 above the 8080 set.</p>

<p>The processor has seven 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Processor_register" title="Processor register">registers</a> accessible to the programmer, named A, B, C, D, E, H, and L, where A is the 8-bit accumulator and the other six can be used as independent byte-registers or as three 16-bit register pairs, BC, DE, and HL, depending on the particular instruction. Some instructions use HL as a (limited) 16-bit accumulator. As in the 8080, the contents of the memory address pointed to by HL could be accessed as pseudo register M. It also has a 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Program_counter" title="Program counter">program counter</a> and a 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Stack-based_memory_allocation" title="Stack-based memory allocation">stack pointer</a> to memory (replacing the 8008's internal <a href="/wiki/Stack_(data_structure)" title="Stack (data structure)" class="mw-redirect">stack</a>). Instructions such as PUSH PSW, POP PSW affected the Program Status Word (accumulator and flags). The accumulator stores the results of arithmetic and logical operations, and the flags register bits (sign, zero, auxiliary carry, parity, and carry flags) are set or cleared according to the results of these operations.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commands.2Finstructions">Commands/instructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commands/instructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As in many other 8-bit processors, all instructions are encoded in a single byte (including register-numbers, but excluding immediate data), for simplicity. Some of them are followed by one or two bytes of data, which could be an immediate operand, a memory address, or a port number. A NOP "no operation" instruction exists, but does not modify any of the registers or flags. Like larger processors, it has CALL and RET instructions for multi-level procedure calls and returns (which can be conditionally executed, like jumps) and instructions to save and restore any 16-bit register-pair on the machine stack. There are also eight one-byte call instructions (RST) for subroutines located at the fixed addresses 00h, 08h, 10h,...,38h. These were intended to be supplied by external hardware in order to invoke a corresponding interrupt-service routine, but are also often employed as fast system calls. The most sophisticated command was XTHL, which is used for exchanging the register pair HL with the value stored at the address indicated by the stack pointer.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="8-bit_instructions">8-bit instructions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 8-bit instructions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most 8-bit operations work on the 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Accumulator_(computing)" title="Accumulator (computing)">accumulator</a> (the A register). For two operand 8-bit operations, the other operand can be either an immediate value, another 8-bit register, or a memory cell addressed by the 16-bit register pair HL. Direct copying is supported between any two 8-bit registers and between any 8-bit register and a HL-addressed memory cell. Due to the regular encoding of the MOV-instruction (using a quarter of available opcode space) there are redundant codes to copy a register into itself (MOV B,B, for instance), which are of little use, except for delays. However, what would have been a copy from the HL-addressed cell into itself (i.e., MOV M,M) instead encodes the <a href="/wiki/HLT" title="HLT">HLT</a> instruction, halting execution until an external reset or interrupt occurred (providing interrupts were enabled).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="16-bit_operations">16-bit operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 16-bit operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although the 8085 is an 8-bit processor, it has some 16-bit operations. Any of the three 16-bit register pairs (BC, DE, HL or SP) could be loaded with an immediate 16-bit value (using LXI), incremented or decremented (using INX and DCX), or added to HL (using DAD). LHLD loaded HL from directly-addressed memory and SHLD stored HL likewise. The XCHG operation exchanges the values of HL and DE. Adding HL to itself performs a 16-bit arithmetical left shift with one instruction. The only 16 bit instruction that affects any flag was DAD (adding HL to BC, DE, HL or SP), which updates the carry flag to facilitate 24-bit or larger additions and left shifts (for a <a href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a> <a href="/wiki/Mantissa_of_a_floating_point_number" title="Mantissa of a floating point number" class="mw-redirect">mantissa</a> for instance). Adding the stack pointer to HL is useful for indexing variables in (recursive) stack frames. A stack frame can be allocated using DAD SP and SPHL, and a branch to a computed pointer can be done with PCHL. These abilities make it feasible to compile languages such as <a href="/wiki/PL/M" title="PL/M">PL/M</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>, or <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a> with 16-bit variables and produce 8085 machine code.</p>
<p>Subtraction and bitwise logical operations on 16 bits is done in 8-bit steps. Operations that have to be implemented by program code (subroutine libraries) included comparisons of signed integers as well as multiply and divide.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Input.2Foutput_scheme">Input/output scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Input/output scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 8085 supported up to 256 <a href="/wiki/Input/output" title="Input/output">input/output</a> (I/O) ports, accessed via dedicated Input/Output instructions—taking port addresses as operands. This I/O mapping scheme was regarded as an advantage, as it freed up the processor's limited address space. The IN and OUT instructions are used to read and write I/O port data.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_system">Development system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Development system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Intel produced a series of development systems for the 8080 and 8085, known as the MDS-80 Microprocessor System. The original development system had an 8080 processor. Later 8085 and 8086 support was added including ICE (<a href="/wiki/In-circuit_emulator" title="In-circuit emulator">in-circuit emulators</a>). It was a large and heavy desktop box, about a 20" cube (in the Intel corporate blue color) which included a CPU, monitor, and a single 8 inch floppy disk drive. Later an external box was available with two more floppy drives. It ran the <a href="/wiki/ISIS_(operating_system)" title="ISIS (operating system)">ISIS</a> operating system and could also operate an <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulator</a> pod and an external <a href="/wiki/EPROM" title="EPROM">EPROM</a> programmer. This unit used the Multibus card cage which was intended just for the development system. A surprising number of spare card cages and processors were being sold, leading to the development of the Multibus as a separate product.</p>
<p>The later iPDS was a portable unit, about 8" x 16" x 20", with a handle. It had a small green screen, a keyboard built into the top, a 5¼ inch floppy disk drive, and ran the ISIS-II operating system. It could also accept a second 8085 processor, allowing a limited form of multi-processor operation where both processors ran simultaneously and independently. The screen and keyboard could be switched between them, allowing programs to be assembled on one processor (large programs took awhile) while files were edited in the other. It had a bubble memory option and various programming modules, including EPROM and Intel 8048 and 8051 programming modules which were plugged into the side, replacing stand-alone device programmers. In addition to an 8080/8085 assembler, Intel produced a number of compilers including <a href="/wiki/PL/M" title="PL/M">PL/M-80</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pascal_programming_language" title="Pascal programming language" class="mw-redirect">Pascal</a> languages, and a set of tools for linking and statically locating programs to enable them to be burnt into <a href="/wiki/EPROM" title="EPROM">EPROMs</a> and used in <a href="/wiki/Embedded_system" title="Embedded system">embedded systems</a>.</p>
<p>A lower cost SDK-85 System Design Kit board was provided with an 8085 CPU, 8355 ROM containing a debugging monitor program, 8155 RAM and 22 I/O, 8279 hex keypad and 8-digit 7-segment LED, TTY (Teletype) 20&#160;mA current loop serial interface. Pads were available for one more 2Kx8 8755 EPROM and another 256&#160;byte RAM 8155 I/O Timer/Counter could be optionally added. All data, control and address signals were available on dual pin headers and a large prototype area was provided.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the extensive use of 8085 in various applications, the microprocessor is provided with an instruction set which consists of various instructions such as MOV, ADD, SUB, JMP, etc. These instructions are written in the form of a program which is used to perform various operations such as branching, addition, subtraction, <a href="/wiki/Bitwise_operation" title="Bitwise operation">bitwise logical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bit_shifting" title="Bit shifting" class="mw-redirect">bit shift</a> operations. More complex operations and other arithmetic operations must be implemented in software. For example, multiplication is implemented using a <a href="/wiki/Multiplication_algorithm" title="Multiplication algorithm">multiplication algorithm</a>.</p>
<p>The 8085 processor was used in a few early personal computers, for example, the <a href="/wiki/TRS-80_Model_100_line" title="TRS-80 Model 100 line" class="mw-redirect">TRS-80 Model 100 line</a> used an OKI manufactured 80C85 (MSM80C85ARS). The <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> version 80C85 of the NMOS/HMOS 8085 processor has several manufacturers. Some manufacturers provide variants with additional functions such as additional instructions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Rad-hard" title="Rad-hard" class="mw-redirect">rad-hard</a> version of the 8085 has been in on-board instrument data processors for several <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and <a href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA" class="mw-redirect">ESA</a> space physics missions in the 1990s and early 2000s, including <a href="/wiki/CRRES" title="CRRES">CRRES</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polar_(satellite)" title="Polar (satellite)">Polar</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=FAST_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="FAST (satellite) (page does not exist)">FAST</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cluster_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cluster (satellite) (page does not exist)">Cluster</a>, <a href="/wiki/HESSI" title="HESSI" class="mw-redirect">HESSI</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Sojourner</a> Mars Rover,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/THEMIS_(satellite)" title="THEMIS (satellite)" class="mw-redirect">THEMIS</a>. The Swiss company SAIA used the 8085 and the 8085-2 as the CPUs of their PCA1 line of <a href="/wiki/Programmable_logic_controller" title="Programmable logic controller">programmable logic controllers</a> during the 1980s.</p>
<p>Pro-Log Corp. put the 8085 and supporting hardware on an STD Bus format card containing CPU, RAM, sockets for ROM/EPROM, I/O and external bus interfaces. The included Instruction Set Reference Card used entirely different mnemonics for the Intel 8085 CPU, as the product was a direct competitor to Intel's <a href="/wiki/Multibus" title="Multibus">Multibus</a> card offerings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MCS-85_family">MCS-85 family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: MCS-85 family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_use">Educational use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intel_8085&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Educational use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many engineering schools<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> the 8085 processor is used in introductory microprocessor courses. Trainer kits composed of a printed circuit board, 8085, and supporting hardware are offered by various companies. These kits usually include complete documentation allowing a student to go from solder to assembly language programming in a single course. Also the architecture of this and the associated instruction set is easy for a student to understand.</p>
